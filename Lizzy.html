  • Research Gap: Despite extensive research on animal behavior, the tendency of lizards to switch from quadrupedal to bipedal stances while running remains puzzling.
  • Project Purpose: Investigate variations in energy consumption in lizards transitioning to a bipedal gait before natural rearing occurs.
  • Background: Prior research indicates that some lizards actively transition to bipedal gaits, but the advantages of this behavior over natural rearing are unclear.
  • Hypothesis: Lizards might achieve lower energy consumption by initiating active rearing during motion.
  • Experimentation Approach: Utilize a lizard-inspired robot capable of modeling both passive and active rearing to determine the energetic advantage of active rearing.

Design of Quadrupedal to Bipedal switching bio-inspired robot

  • Co-designed a robot that could switch from quadrupedal to bipedal gait during the run using its tail and trunk, the robot design drew inspiration from
    lizards and was used to test multiple research hypotheses.
  • Initial design calculations and experimentation: I did the initial calculation required to base the design on firm mathematical foundations, the calculations
    included calculating acceleration requirements for initiating rearing, minimum rpm of the legs required to stabilize the robot and motor selection calculations.
    Co-designed experiments and built simple test setups to conduct the experiments to quantify the behavior of Rhex legs and abs motor behavior.
  • I designed a PD controller to control the tail and stabilize the robot body during rearing and bipedal running, I codesigned the overall control flow of the robot
    and also implemented it on an arduino Mega and integrated it with IMU and Dynamixel servo motors.

Designed of Quadrupedal to Bipedal switching bio-inspired robot

  • Control Architecture: Features separate designs for two operation modes: passive and active.
    • Passive Mode: The robot rears using reaction torque and inertia without actuation, where the tail is used only for stabilizing posture.
    • Active Transition Phase: Involves sequential actuation of the 'Abs' motor to lift the front wheels, a solenoid to trigger rearing,
      and the tail for executing the rearing motion.
  • Stabilization in Both Modes: Uses the tail for stabilization in both active and passive rearing modes, preventing toppling.
  • Proportional-Derivative Controller: A current IMU reading is compared against a preset target rearing angle, and the tail is actuated based on the error calculation.
